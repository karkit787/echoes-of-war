# History Content Context

## Important note

This file contains only the historical content scope already discussed in the project chats. When adding specific dates, treaty clauses, figures, or curriculum claims, verify them against reliable historical sources or the intended Singapore secondary History syllabus/materials. Do not invent details for the sake of drama.

## Overall history scope

The game teaches History through the events around World War II, especially the causes and developments leading up to it. The player travels through key events and must ensure that history unfolds as originally recorded.

## Historical design rule

The project is not an alternate-history power fantasy. The player can investigate, influence conversations, and repair unstable details, but major historical anchor events should remain aligned with real history.

Use instability for learning:

- If the player misunderstands causes, the timeline may distort.
- If the player ignores consequences, trust or stability may fall.
- If the player reasons accurately, the timeline becomes clearer.

## Chapter 0: Prologue

### Purpose

Introduce the sci-fi framing, the player’s role, the damaged memory premise, and the companion fragments.

### Starting scene

The player wakes in an abandoned building. This location will be the laboratory in the future, but in the past it is empty, damaged, or unfamiliar.

### Key beats

1. Player wakes with a severe headache.
2. Environmental clues suggest the place is connected to the future lab.
3. Flashbacks show the time-travel experiment.
4. The experiment fails and sends the player into the past.
5. The player’s mind fractures into companion traits.
6. The timeline instability threat is introduced.
7. Player receives the first objective: survive, investigate, and repair the path back.

### Gameplay goals

- Teach basic controls and interaction.
- Introduce observation/investigation.
- Trigger first companion dialogue.
- Establish that memory and history are unreliable unless carefully examined.

### Possible decision points

- How does the player respond to waking up disoriented?
- Which environmental clue does the player inspect first?
- Does the player trust a memory fragment immediately or question it?
- How does the player interpret the first timeline distortion?

## Chapter 1: After World War I

### Focus

Aims and terms of the Paris Peace Conference and its immediate impact on Europe in the 1920s.

### Learning objectives

The player should understand:

- That different powers entered the peace process with different aims.
- That treaty terms were shaped by competing desires such as security, punishment, compensation, and stability.
- That the immediate post-war settlement affected Europe politically, economically, and socially in the 1920s.
- That the consequences of the settlement helped shape later tensions.

### Narrative frame

The player arrives around the aftermath of World War I and must investigate why the peace settlement matters. Timeline instability may appear as altered documents, missing delegates, distorted public reactions, or contradictory memories.

### Key scene types

#### 1. Arrival in post-war Europe

Purpose: Establish devastation, uncertainty, and the need for peace.

Player activity:

- Observe civilians, soldiers, damaged surroundings, or newspapers.
- Identify that Europe is trying to rebuild after war.

Companion emphasis:

- Empathy highlights human cost.
- Logic introduces cause and consequence.

#### 2. Delegation aims

Purpose: Explore different aims at the peace conference.

Player activity:

- Compare what different leaders or countries want.
- Identify tension between punishment and lasting stability.

Companion emphasis:

- Scepticism questions political promises.
- Memory recalls that peace terms can create future resentment.

#### 3. Treaty terms

Purpose: Understand that treaty terms are not isolated facts but decisions with consequences.

Player activity:

- Match terms to intended aims.
- Predict possible reactions.
- Decide how to prevent a distorted treaty record from changing the timeline.

Companion emphasis:

- Logic evaluates timeline stability.
- Conscience reflects on fairness and suffering.

#### 4. Immediate impact in the 1920s

Purpose: Show how post-war settlements affected societies and politics.

Player activity:

- Interview civilians, veterans, officials, or students.
- Connect treaty consequences to instability in Europe.

Companion emphasis:

- Empathy humanizes consequences.
- Scepticism challenges simplified explanations.

### Suggested decision examples

#### Decision: Advising a delegate

Prompt:

> “A delegate asks what kind of peace will prevent another war. What do you advise, and why?”

Expected reasoning:

- A good answer recognises competing aims and long-term consequences.
- A weak answer focuses only on revenge or only on idealism without considering political realities.

#### Decision: Correcting a distorted document

Prompt:

> “The treaty record has changed. One term is missing, and the crowd outside is reacting to a false rumour. What do you investigate first?”

Expected reasoning:

- A good answer checks evidence before acting.
- Scepticism should challenge rumours.
- Memory may recall what the document should contain, but the player should still verify.

#### Decision: Responding to public anger

Prompt:

> “A crowd blames one group for all suffering after the war. How do you respond without worsening the instability?”

Expected reasoning:

- A good answer avoids simplistic blame, recognises suffering, and redirects to evidence.
- Conscience and Scepticism should be active.

## Content writing principles

- Start each scene with a learning objective.
- Tie every major decision to a historical concept.
- Use civilians and ordinary people to show impact, not just leaders.
- Avoid making the player feel they can single-handedly rewrite world history.
- End scenes with short reflection: what happened, why it mattered, what the player learned.

## Historical accuracy checklist

Before accepting new historical content, confirm:

- The event and date are accurate.
- The figures involved are appropriate for the scene.
- The terms and consequences are not exaggerated or oversimplified.
- The content does not imply false causality.
- The educational point is clear.
- Sensitive issues are treated neutrally and respectfully.

## MOE syllabus anchor

The project now has an explicit curriculum source: the MOE Upper Secondary History / Humanities (History) Teaching and Learning Syllabuses, implementation from the 2023 Secondary Three cohort.

Use `docs/codex/MOE_SYLLABUS_ALIGNMENT.md` as the first reference before expanding history content.

Key alignment points:

- The game should support historical inquiry, not only factual recall.
- Chapter 1 maps directly to the syllabus topic: aims and terms of the Paris Peace Conference and immediate impact on Europe in the 1920s.
- Future content can map to the rise of authoritarian regimes, Nazi Germany, Militarist Japan for O-Level, and key developments leading to WWII in Europe and Asia-Pacific.
- Detailed study of WWII military campaigns is not required and should not dominate the game.
- Each scene should connect at least one historical concept: chronology, evidence, accounts, causation, change and continuity, significance, historical empathy, or diversity.
